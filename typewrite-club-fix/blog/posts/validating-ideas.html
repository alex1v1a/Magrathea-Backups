How We Validated Reel Reviews Before Writing Code

Customer development techniques that saved us months of wasted effort.

Paper Trail Team Founders

The idea for Reel Reviews came from a simple observation: we were spending way too much time reading positive reviews to find the one negative nugget that would actually tell us something useful. But was this a problem others had? We didn't know. So we spent two weeks finding out before writing a single line of code.

Step 1: The Problem Interview

We started with 30-minute interviews. Not pitches—interviews. We talked to 40 people about how they make decisions about restaurants, hotels, and services. We didn't mention our idea. We just listened.

Key questions we asked:

  • Walk me through the last time you tried a new restaurant. How did you decide?
  • What do you look for in reviews? What makes a review helpful?
  • Have you ever been disappointed after reading mostly positive reviews?
  • How do you filter through review sites today?

The pattern was clear: people were frustrated. They felt like positive reviews were inflated, incentivized, or fake. But they also felt guilty for focusing on negative feedback. One person called it "looking for problems." Another said, "I know I shouldn't be so negative, but..."

Step 2: The Solution Sketch

After validating the problem, we sketched solutions. Not mockups—hand-drawn sketches on paper. We showed them to the same people we'd interviewed.

The first sketches were wrong. We showed a traditional review app with a "sort by negative" option. People didn't get it. "Why would I want to see only bad reviews?" they asked.

The breakthrough came when we reframed it. Instead of "negative reviews," we called them "honest reviews" or "what to watch out for." The positioning mattered as much as the feature.

Step 3: The Fake Door Test

Before building, we created a landing page with a "Download the App" button. The app didn't exist. When people clicked, they saw: "Coming soon! Leave your email to be first to try it."

We ran $100 of targeted ads to people interested in restaurant reviews and local discovery. The click-through rate was 4.2%. More importantly, 60% of clickers left their email. That was enough signal to proceed.

Step 4: The Concierge MVP

Our first "product" wasn't an app. It was a simple form where people could text us a restaurant name, and we'd manually compile the negative reviews from various sources and text them back.

It was janky. It took us 10-15 minutes per request. But it proved the value proposition. People used it. They came back. They told friends. We processed 200 requests over two weeks before shutting it down to build the real thing.

What We Learned

Validation isn't about certainty. It's about reducing risk. We didn't prove Reel Reviews would be a huge success. We just proved that:

  1. The problem was real and painful
  2. Our framing of the solution resonated
  3. People would take action (click, sign up, use a manual service)

That's enough to justify building. If any of those three hadn't been true, we would have pivoted or killed the idea—saving months of development time.

The Cost of Skipping Validation

We've made this mistake before. Our first app (now sunsetted) was built on assumptions. We spent six months developing something nobody wanted. The technical execution was perfect. The market fit was nonexistent.

The two weeks we spent validating Reel Reviews felt slow in the moment. In retrospect, they were the fastest weeks we spent. They gave us confidence. They gave us customer quotes to guide development. They gave us a waitlist of excited users before launch.

"Build it and they will come" is a lie. Build it after they ask for it. That's the difference between product-market fit and product-market hope.

If you're working on something new, take the time to validate. Your future self will thank you.
